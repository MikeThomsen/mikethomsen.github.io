Countering burnout in open source development

Posted on Sat 21 November 2020 in opinion

But open source success, Thornton quickly found, has a dark side. He felt inundated. Countless people wrote him and Otto every week with bug reports, demands for new features, questions, praise. Thornton would finish his day job and then spend four or five hours every night frantically working on Bootstrap—managing queries, writing new code. “I couldn't grab dinner with someone after work,” he says, because he felt like he'd be letting users down: I shouldn't be out enjoying myself. I should be working on Bootstrap!

“The feeling that I had was guilt,” he says. He kept at it, and nine years later he and Otto are still heading up Bootstrap, along with a small group of core contributors. But the stress has been bad enough that he often thought of bailing.

Wired

One of the benefits to the "Apache model" is that most popular Apache products have a commercial vendor backing the team. Sometimes two or more. It's actually quite easy to tell users who are insistent or demanding to go buy commercial support if it's that important to them because it's an option.

In the case of the Bootstrap team, they should have set up subscriptions, donations, etc. a long time ago. Their product is immensely valuable, and it's used in so many places, providing so much value that they've earned it 10x over. It's also perfectly reasonable for them to go the community and tell them "support us because we're burning out." This isn't a hobbyist project, it's something that is used everywhere from startups to Fortune 500 companies.

At the end of the day, the issue here is that projects like Bootstrap, the Apache and CNCF ones, Linux kernel, etc. are not "hobbyist" projects. They provide immense value in the real world. There should be an expectation that if your daily work flow, personal computer, etc. is driven by this software you give something back--even if that's just $10/quarter to a foundation to pay bug bounties.
